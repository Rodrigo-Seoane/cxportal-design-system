## Overview

Navigation shows users where they are in the product and where they can go. The Design System vertical navigation supports a two-level hierarchy — group headers at the top level and sub-items nested below — providing access to multi-section application areas while keeping the overall structure compact and scannable.

> **Navigation goals:** Let the user know exactly where they are in the product and take them to where they want to go quickly and effortlessly.

## When to use

- When the application has **6 or more distinct destinations**
- When the structure has **1–3 levels of hierarchy** (prefer 2 for clarity)
- When side context — seeing the menu while viewing content — improves orientation
- When the same nav is shared across many different views and sections

**Do not use** the vertical nav as the primary navigation when the application has fewer than 6 pages. Prefer a top navigation bar for flat, few-page structures.

## Principles

### Discoverability
Users can locate the information they want. Design the information architecture to be as light-weight and inclusive as possible.

### Efficiency
- **Multiple access points** — multiple links to the same destination where helpful
- **Shortcuts** — provide shortcuts to content, such as related links
- **Escape pods** — return to the home page by clicking the logo so users can restart their journey

## Anatomy

- **Sidebar panel** — Fixed left panel, 240px wide, dark background (`#050326`)
- **Brand header** — Logo mark + product name + sub-title. 64px tall.
- **Group header (Menu Item)** — 48px tall. Leading icon (20px, thin weight) + label + expand/collapse caret. Toggles the sub-item list.
- **Sub-items (Sub Menu Item)** — 40px tall. 48px left indent. Label only (no icon). Status badge optional.
- **Footer** — Bottom-anchored utility links (e.g. Access Management) + version mark.

## Variants

### States

| State | Background | Label weight | Use for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Default** | Transparent | Light (300) | Resting, not interacted |
| **Hover** | `#4285f4` (primary blue) | Light (300) | Mouse-over — preview of selection |
| **Active** | `#3264b8` (primary dark) | SemiBold (600) | Currently selected / focused page |
| **Disabled** | Transparent | Light (300) | Feature not available; text `#808080` |

### Item types

| Type | Height | Left padding | Right padding | Context |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Menu Item** (group header) | 48px | 12px | 12px | Top-level group, always has icon + caret |
| **Sub Menu Item** | 40px | 48px | 24px | Child route, text only |

## Information Architecture

Two recommended approaches for organizing navigation groups:

1. **Categorize by topic** — group content directly related to the service or content scope of the site
2. **Categorize by tasks** — group by cooperation modalities, contacting cooperation specialists, signing processes, business operations, and customer service

The importance of each menu item is less affected by the arrangement in a side navigation than in a top bar — all items share equivalent visual weight until one is active.

> **Note:** It is not recommended to use distinct categories for color/products unless there are better visibility cues that support frequent use.

## Do's & Don'ts

<DosDonts>
  <Do description="Keep group labels short — one or two words that clearly identify the section. Users scan labels; they shouldn't need to read them carefully to understand the scope." />
  <Dont description="Use verb phrases like 'Manage Campaigns' or 'View Insights' for group headers. Noun phrases ('Campaigns', 'Insights') are faster to scan and match the user's mental model of sections, not tasks." />
</DosDonts>

<DosDonts>
  <Do description="Auto-expand the group that contains the current active page when the user first lands on or navigates to that route. It reduces orientation effort." />
  <Dont description="Expand all groups by default. Showing all sub-items at once defeats the purpose of collapsible groups and overwhelms the visual hierarchy." />
</DosDonts>

<DosDonts>
  <Do description="Limit sub-items per group to 5–8 items. If a group grows beyond that, consider splitting it into two groups or adding a second level of hierarchy." />
  <Dont description="Add more than 3 levels of nesting. The current component supports 2 levels (group + sub-item). Deep nesting creates orientation problems and makes the back-path unclear." />
</DosDonts>

<DosDonts>
  <Do description="Keep disabled nav items visible in the sidebar so users understand the product's full scope. Pair them with surrounding context or a tooltip explaining why the section is locked." />
  <Dont description="Remove disabled items from the nav. Users lose awareness of capabilities that exist but are not yet available to them — especially important in tiered products." />
</DosDonts>

<DosDonts>
  <Do description="Use the thin-weight icon style (20px, thin) for group header icons. This maintains visual lightness and keeps icons from competing with the label text." />
  <Dont description="Use filled or bold icons in the sidebar. They create too much visual weight and make the dark-background panel feel cluttered when multiple groups are visible." />
</DosDonts>

## Accessibility

- The `<aside>` landmark element communicates the sidebar's role to screen readers.
- Group headers are rendered as `<button>` elements so they receive keyboard focus and `Enter`/`Space` toggles the group.
- Sub-item links are native `<a>` elements for full keyboard navigation.
- The active item reflects the current page — combine with `aria-current="page"` on the active link for screen-reader announcements.
- The collapsed/expanded state of group headers should be communicated with `aria-expanded` on the button.
- Avoid relying on colour alone to communicate the active state — the SemiBold font weight provides a second visual cue.
